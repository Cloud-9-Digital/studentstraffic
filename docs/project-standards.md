# Project Standards

This document stores working standards for Students Traffic so content, UX, and conversion decisions stay consistent across the project.

For the authoritative, non-editorial catalogue-content policy, evidence model, fee states,
optional-section gate and CTA contract, see [`content-framework.md`](./content-framework.md).

## Purpose

Students Traffic should position itself as a trusted study-abroad partner that helps students move toward their dream country, dream university, and best-fit admission outcome.

This website is not a pure editorial publication whose job ends at informing the reader. It is a hybrid platform:
- information and research
- counseling and shortlist guidance
- application and admissions support
- lead capture and service conversion

Students Traffic serves a global audience. Indian students and parents remain an important audience,
but country, university and programme coverage must not be limited to Indian eligibility or Indian
search intent.

That means every serious page should help the reader understand their options and also make it natural to take the next step with Students Traffic.

The brand promise should feel:
- Aspirational
- Supportive
- Expert
- Commercial without sounding desperate or spammy

## Content standards

For admissions, scholarship, country, and decision-stage pages:
- Write for real students and parents, not for search engines alone.
- Prefer authoritative, student-facing language over developer, strategist, or SEO-jargon language.
- Use strong informational content first, then layer in commercial intent naturally.
- Avoid filler, thin repetition, and generic motivational copy.
- Keep pages evergreen where possible; avoid baking in years unless the topic is genuinely cycle-dependent and reviewed.
- When facts are time-sensitive, frame them carefully and encourage verification from official sources instead of implying static permanence.
- Do not make the page feel like a detached editorial article whose only job is to publish information.
- Do not overexpose citations, source mechanics, or update-process language in user-facing copy unless the page truly needs it.
- Avoid sounding like a research portal, newsroom, or encyclopedia even when the underlying work is heavily researched.
- The content should feel like expert guidance from a company that can actually help the student act on that information.

## Conversion standards

Students Traffic should be clearly present throughout the page as the expert partner that can help students:
- choose the right country
- shortlist the right universities
- understand scholarship fit
- improve admission readiness
- move toward their dream university or dream destination
- start their application
- take counseling
- complete admissions with Students Traffic where relevant

But the commercial bridge must be credible:
- Do not turn every section into a sales pitch.
- Use upsell language where it supports user intent.
- Prefer trust-building calls to action such as shortlist help, profile evaluation, scholarship-fit review, admissions counseling, or application support.
- The page should feel helpful first and commercial second, while still making the service offer visible.
- We are not only educating the reader; we are also inviting them to work with Students Traffic.
- Commercial intent should be woven into the page as a natural next step, not bolted on awkwardly at the end.

## Brand voice standards

The website should sound:
- transparent
- honest
- trustworthy
- authoritative
- commercially capable
- deeply involved in helping students achieve real outcomes

The website should not sound:
- like a neutral editorial magazine
- like a passive education blog
- like a citation-heavy research archive
- like a generic lead-generation site with shallow sales copy

The ideal voice is expert guidance with commercial confidence. We know the information, we explain it clearly, and we are also ready to help the student with counseling, applications, and admissions.

## Scholarship-page standards

Scholarship pages should:
- explain what scholarships really mean in that country
- separate full funding, partial funding, and affordability myths
- clarify what students still need to pay
- include named scholarship examples where helpful
- explain which student profiles are more competitive
- connect scholarship interest to admission planning and country-university fit

## Writing standards

- Use clear, natural English.
- Avoid internal phrasing like "this page converts well" or "this keyword works because".
- Avoid writing that sounds like an editorial strategy memo.
- Prefer concrete advice, realistic expectations, and calm authority.
- Use globally clear English by default. Add India-specific guidance only where it is relevant and
  label it as such; do not make it the assumed eligibility rule for every reader.
- Do not write like a journalist explaining a topic from a distance.
- Write like an expert team that understands the topic and can actively help the student move forward.
- When useful, connect informational clarity to action with language such as shortlist, counseling, admission support, application help, or fit assessment.
- Keep the tone trustworthy and grounded; do not oversell beyond what the page can honestly support.

## UX standards for long-form pages

- Keep above-the-fold sections tight.
- Reduce unnecessary scrolling where possible.
- Use accordions or collapsible navigation when a page gets long.
- Make sure headings, badges, and card elements never overlap.
- Favor readability and scanning over decorative complexity.

## Operational note

This file should be updated whenever the team makes a new project-wide decision about content, conversion style, trust signals, or page structure.
